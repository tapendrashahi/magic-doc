Tissues

- The word tissue is derived from texere (Latin: 'weave') ✓ Meaning of texere: Cells that form tissues are woven together. - Tissue: A group or layer of specialised cells with a similar structure and perform a specific function. ✓ A level of organisation in multicellular organism ✓ An assembly of similar cells and their extracellular matrix from same embryonic origin ✓ An aggregate of cells usually of a particular kind together with their intercellular substance that form one of the structural materials of life - Tissue shows an inflammatory reaction to an infection or injury. - Present in the multicellular animals from the Phylum Coelenterata to Phylum Chordata [No true tissues are present in Phylum Porifera although cells are present and they are cellular-grade] - Groups of tissues: Produce organs - Groups of organs: Produce systems - Groups of the systems: Produce a single body

History of Tissues/Histology

- Marcello Malpighi (1628-1694): Founder/Pioneer of Microscopical Anatomy, Founder of Histology; Father of Histology; Father of Physiology and Embryology - Malpighi: First observation of capillaries in animals and discovered the link between arteries and veins - Marie Francois Xavier Bichat (1771-1802): Father of Modern Histology - Bichat: Coined the term 'Tissue' in 1801 - Bichat's Contribution: Identified 21 different types of tissues based on their physical \& functional features without help of microscopes - 'Histology' coined by: Dr. Karl Mayer (1819) - Book of Mayer: On histology and a new division of the tissues of the human body

Epithelial Tissues

Epithelial tissues: Group of cells that form limiting and lining membranes and cover the free surface of an organ, body surface, cavities, \& form glands - Contain two distinct surfaces (Apical or Free surface \& Basal or Fixed surface) ✓ Apical surface: Exposed to the body cavity ✓ Basal surface: Near the underlying tissue ✓ Tight junctions: Area between apical \& basal surfaces - Epithelial surface supported on the basal surface by a basement membrane (basal lamina) ✓ Basement membrane: Thin layer of a specialised exracellular matrix ✓ Molecular composition of basement membrane: Collagen IV + Proteoglycans + Nodegen complex - Derived from all 3 germinal layers (Ectoderm + Mesoderm + Endoderm) ✓ Ectoderm: Skin; Endoderm: Linings of respiratory \& gastrointestinal tract; Mesoderm: Renal epithelium - Avascular or non-vascular due to lack of blood vessels - Depend on underlying connective tissues with capillary beds for nutrition \& removal of waste products - Easily regenerate, i.e., sunburn and skinned knee regeneration. - Divide throughout their life - Provided with microvilli (Brushbordered), Stercocilia, Kinocilia, or Flagella - Bound together in sheets of tissues via forces of interactions: i. Tight junctions: Form a watertight seal between cells ✓ Tight seal is formed by transmembrane proteins (Claudins + Occludins) ✓ Prevents leakage of molecules between cells (Seal-Forming Junctions) ii. Gap Junctions (Communication Junctions): Channels for direct communication \& transport of small molecules between cells iii. Adherens Junction: Link the cell membrane \& cytoskeletal elements (Actin) within the cells ✓ Contains a cadherin-catenin complex iv. Desmosomes: Act like 'Spot Welds' or 'Rivets'[Provide strong adhesion between cells] ✓ Use membrane protein (Cadherins) and Intermediate filaments to join cells - Examples: Hard and horny chitin of insects, scales of fish, horny plates and scales of reptiles, feathers of birds, and hair and nails of mammals Location: Capillary bed; Female reproductive organs; Kidney; Major cavities of the body; Skin; Bile duct and salivary glands; The lining of alveoli or air sacs; Lining of the nose, ear, and eye; Oesophagus, stomach, small intestine, and pancreas

1. Holocrine gland

- Secretions in cytoplasm - Secretion released by plasmalemma rupture - Release of secretions with cell death - Example: Sebaceous glands, tarsal glands 2.
