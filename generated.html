1. Complex number :

Any ordered pair of real numbers ( \(\mathrm{a}, \mathrm{b}\) ) is known as a complex number. It is denoted by \(z\) or \(w\) and given by \(z=(a, b)=a+i b\) $$ \text { where } \begin{aligned} & a=\operatorname{Re}(z) \\ & b=\operatorname{Im}(z) \end{aligned} $$ i → imaginary unit of complex number. Note : \(-\sqrt{-1}\) is denoted by i and is pronounced by iota.

Examples :

$$ \begin{aligned} & z=(2+3 i) \\ & w=(-1+2 i) \text { etc. } \end{aligned} $$

Thus, \(C=\{(a, b): a, b \in R\}\) !

Complex Plane

Every real number is a complex number : \(x\) is a real number we can write \(x\) as : \(\mathrm{x}=\mathrm{x}+0 \mathrm{i}\) Thus \(\mathrm{R} \subset \mathrm{C}\) (Real number system is the subset of complex number system) Where \(R\) is the set of all real numbers and \(C\) is the set of all complex numbers.

2. Operations of complex numbers:

(1) Equality:

Any two complex number \(\mathrm{z}=(\mathrm{a}, \mathrm{b})\) and \(\mathrm{w}=(\mathrm{c}, \mathrm{d})\) are said to be equal if $$ \begin{aligned} & a=c \\ & \text { and } b=d \end{aligned} $$ i.e., real parts and imaginary parts are equal.

(2) Addition :

\(\mathrm{z}=(\mathrm{a}, \mathrm{b})\) and \(\mathrm{w}=(\mathrm{c}, \mathrm{d})\) be any two complex numbers then $$ \begin{aligned} z+w & =(a, b)+(c, d) \\ & =(a+c, b+d) \end{aligned} $$

Example:- $$ \begin{aligned} \mathrm{z} & =(3,4) \\ \mathrm{w} & =(2,1) \\ \mathrm{z}+\mathrm{w} & =(3+2,4+1) \\ & =(5,5) \end{aligned} $$

(3) Multiplication :

If \(\mathrm{z}=(\mathrm{a}, \mathrm{b})\) and \(\mathrm{w}=(\mathrm{c}, \mathrm{d})\) be any two complex numbers then $$ \begin{aligned} z \cdot w & =(a, b) \cdot(c, d) \\ & =(a c-b d, a d+b c) \end{aligned} $$

Example: $$ \begin{aligned} \mathrm{z} & =(2,3) \\ \mathrm{w} & =(2,5) \\ \text { then } \mathrm{z} \cdot \mathrm{w} & =(2.3-3.5,2.5+2.3) \\ \vdots & =(-9,16) \end{aligned} $$ (4) Division : let \(\mathrm{z}=\mathrm{a}+\mathrm{ib}\) \(\mathrm{w}=\mathrm{c}+\mathrm{id}\) be any two complex numbers $$ \begin{aligned} \frac{z}{w} & =\frac{a+i b}{c+i d}=\frac{(a+i b)(c-i d)}{(c+i d)(c-i d)} \\ & =\frac{(a c+b d)+i(b c-a d)}{c^{2}+d^{2}} \\ & =\left(\frac{a c+b d}{c^{2}+d^{2}}\right)+\left(\frac{b c-a d}{c^{2}+d^{2}}\right) \end{aligned} $$ ; Where \(z \neq 0\)

4. Purely real and Purely imaginary

(i) A complex number \(z=a+i b\) is said to be purely real if imaginary part \(b=0\) i.e $$ \begin{aligned} & z=\bar{z} \\ & a+i b=a-i b \\ & 2 i b=0 \\ & b=0 \end{aligned} $$ (ii) A complex number is said to be purely imaginary if real part \(a=0\) i.e $$ \begin{aligned} & z=\bar{z} \\ & a+i b=-(a-i b) \\ & a+i b=-a+i b \\ & 2 a=0 \\ & a=0 \end{aligned} $$

Note: Every imaginary number is a complex number but every complex number is not an imaginary number

7. Conjugate of a complex number:-

Let \(\mathrm{z}=(\mathrm{a}+\mathrm{ib})\) be any complex number then its conjugate denoted by \(\bar{Z}\) is given by $$ \bar{z}=a-i b $$

Example:- $$ \begin{aligned} \mathrm{z} & =3+4 \mathrm{i} \\ \bar{z} & =3-4 i \\ -\overline{7} & =-7 \end{aligned} $$ (the conjugate of real number coincides with itself)

Example:-

The conjugate of the complex number \(\left(\frac{1-i}{1+i}\right)\) is: a) 2 I b) \((1-i)\) c) -I d) i

Solution:- $$ \begin{aligned} \frac{1-i}{1+i} & =\frac{1-i}{1+i} \times \frac{1-i}{1-i} \\ & =\frac{1-2 i+i^{2}}{1-i^{2}} \\ & =\frac{1-2 i-1}{1+1}=-\mathrm{i} \end{aligned} $$

Its conjugate is i .

9. Absolute value (Modulus) of a complex number:-

Let \(z=(a+i b)\) be any complex number. The absolute value of a complex number denoted by \(|z|\) is given by $$ |z|=\sqrt{a^{2}+b^{2}} $$

Example:-

The modulus of \((1+\sqrt{2}+i)\) is a) \(2 \sqrt{2}\) b) \(2+\sqrt{3}\) c) \(\sqrt{4+2 \sqrt{2}}\) d) 1

Solution:-

$$ \begin{aligned} |z| & =\sqrt{(1+\sqrt{2})^{2}+(1)^{2}} \\ & =\sqrt{1+2 \sqrt{2}+2+1} \\ & =\sqrt{4+2 \sqrt{2}} \end{aligned} $$

Example:-

The modulus of \(\frac{a-i b}{a+i b}\) is equals to: a) \(\sqrt{a^{2}+b^{2}}\) b) \((a+b)\) c) 1 d) \(\frac{1}{2}(a+b)\)

$$ \begin{aligned} \mathrm{z} & =\frac{a-i b}{a+i b} \\ \text { Let }|\mathrm{z}| & =\left|\frac{a-i b}{a+i b}\right| \\ & =\frac{\sqrt{a^{2}+(-b)^{2}}}{\sqrt{a^{2}+b^{2}}}=1 \end{aligned} $$

Different forms of complex number:-

a) Cartesian form:-

Complex number in the form \(z=(x+i y)\) is known as cartesian form of complex number Example: $$ z=2+3 i, 1+i \text { etc. } $$ b) Polar Form:-

Complex number in the form \(\mathrm{z}=\mathrm{r}(\cos \theta+\mathrm{i} \sin \theta)\) is known as polar form of complex number \(z=x+i y\). where, \(\mathrm{r}^{2}=\mathrm{x}^{2}+\mathrm{y}^{2}\) and \(\tan \theta=y / x\) Where \(\theta\) is known as argument or amplitude.

Note:- (i) The principal value of argument of \(z\) denoted by argz is given by: $$ \arg (z)=\tan ^{-1}(y / x), z \neq 0(-\pi<\theta \leq \pi) $$ (ii) The principal values of argument of \(Z\) will be \(\theta, \pi-\theta,-\pi+\theta\) and \(-\theta\) according as the point \(z\) lies in the \(1^{\text {st }}, 2^{\text {nd }}, 3^{\text {rd }}\) and \(4^{\text {th }}\) quadrant. (iii) If \(\arg z=0\) or \(\pi\) then \(z\) is purely real. (iv) If \(\arg z=\pi / 2\) or \(-\pi / 2\) then \(z\) is purely imaginary. (v) Argument of complex number 0 is not defined.

Fourth roots of unity:-

(i) Fourth roots of unity are \(\pm 1, \pm \mathrm{i}\) (ii) Sum of fourth roots of unity is 0 , i.e. \(1+\mathrm{i}-1-\mathrm{i}=0\) (iii) Product of fourth roots of unity is -1 i.e \(1 \times-1 \times i \times-i=i^{2}=-1\) (iv) Fourth roots of unity on the Argand plane forms a square. (v) \(n^{\text {th }}\) roots of unity on the 'Argand plane' forms a regular polygon having \(n\) sides. (vi) The area of triangle with vertices are \(z, i z\) and \(z+i z\) is \(\frac{1}{2}|Z|^{2}\). (vii) The sum of \(n, n^{\text {th }}\) roots of unity ( \(\mathrm{n}>1\) ) is zero.

By applying De moivre's theorem, $$ \begin{aligned} & =\cos \frac{2 n \pi}{4}+i \sin \frac{2 n \pi}{4} \\ & =\cos \frac{n \pi}{2}+i \sin \frac{n \pi}{2} \end{aligned} $$

Putting \(\mathrm{n}=0,1,2,3\) we get the required result.

Example:-

The value of \(\frac{a+b w+c w^{2}}{a w+b w^{2}+c}=\) a) 1 b) \(w\) c) \(w^{2}\) d) 0

Solution:-

$$ \frac{a+b w+c w^{2}}{a w+b w^{2}+c} $$

Multiplying both numerator and denominator by w , we get $$ \begin{aligned} & =\frac{a w+b w^{2}+c w^{3}}{w\left(a w+b w^{2}+c\right)} \\ & =\frac{1}{w}\left[\therefore w^{3}=1\right] \\ & =\frac{w^{3}}{w}=w^{2} \end{aligned} $$
