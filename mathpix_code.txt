Circle

\section*{Definition:-}

⇒ A geometrical path traced out by a moving point in a plane such that its distance from some fixed point always remain--s equal or constant is called a circle.

→ The fixed point is called centre of the circle and the constant distance is called the radius of the circle.

→ The outer path is called circumference.

We can also define a circle as:A closed plane figure bounded by a curve such that every point of the curve remains equidistant from a fixed point is called circle.

→ The fixed point is called the centre of the circle, the constant distance is called radius of the circle and the bounding

Curve iscalled the circumference of the circle.

In 'above figure, ' 0 ' is the centre of the circle, $OA=OB=r$ be radius of the circle.

\section*{Chord:-}

A line segment joining any two points of the circumference is called the chord of the circle. In the above figure, $AB, CD, EF$ are chords of the circle.

\section*{Arc}

A part of the circumference of a circle is called an arc.
In figure,

\begin{aligned}

\section*{Diameter:}

The longest chord passing through the centre of a circle is called its diameter. It is twice the radius of the circle.

One of two parts of a circle made by its diameter is said to be a semi-circle.

\section*{Segments}

Two unequal parts of a circle made by a chord are called its segments.
![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/412488ef-565e-4a71-b199-1e462d89fca7-03.jpg?height=427&width=486&top_left_y=1806&top_left_x=39)

Here, the larger one is called major segments and the smaller one is called minor Segment.

\section*{Sector:}
![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/412488ef-565e-4a71-b199-1e462d89fca7-04.jpg?height=518&width=738&top_left_y=478&top_left_x=164)

The area of a circle between any two radii is called its sector. The greater one is called major sector and the smaller one is called minor sector.

\section*{Central angle:}

The angle subtended by an are at the centre of a circle is called its central angle. It is the angle made by any two radii of the circle at its centre.
The central angle and its opposite arc are equal in degree measure.

Here, angle $AOB$ is called the central angle.
Also,
$\angle AOB \cong \widehat{AOB}$ read as the arc $AOB$ equals in degree measure. Where, the symbol $\cong$ read as equal influence.

\section*{Inscribed angle:}

An angle subtended by an are of a circle at its circumference is called an inscribed angle. An inscribed angle is an angle made by any two chords of a circle at its circumference. The inscribed angle is also called an angle at the circumference.
![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/412488ef-565e-4a71-b199-1e462d89fca7-05.jpg?height=506&width=776&top_left_y=1818&top_left_x=0)

\section*{Concentric Circles:}

Two or more circles having same centre is called Concentric circles.

\section*{Intersecting Circles:}

Two circles which intersect each other at two distinct points are called intersecting circles.

\section*{Con-cyclic points:-}

Two or more than two points which lie on the circumference of the same circle are called con-cyclic points.

\section*{Cyclic quadrilaterals:-}
![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/412488ef-565e-4a71-b199-1e462d89fca7-07.jpg?height=452&width=558&top_left_y=219&top_left_x=113)

A quadrilateral whose all vertices lie on the circumference of a circle is called a cyclic quadrilateral.

Given: $O$ is the centre of the circle. The central angle $\angle AOB$ and inscribed angle $\angle ACB$ are based on the same arc $AB$ as shown in figure below:-
![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/412488ef-565e-4a71-b199-1e462d89fca7-15.jpg?height=461&width=405&top_left_y=867&top_left_x=155)

\section*{Proof:}

\begin{tabular}{|l|l|l|}

\hline (2) & $\angle AOD = \angle OAC + \angle OCA$ & Exterior and opposite interior angle of $\triangle AOC$. \\
\hline (3) & In $\triangle BOC$: $\angle BOD = 2\angle OCB$ & Same as above facts and reasons. \\
\hline (4) & $\angle AOD + \angle BOD = 2\angle OCA + 2\angle OCB = 2(\angle OCA + \angle OCB)$ & From statements above. \\
\hline (5) & $\angle AOB = 2\angle ACB$ i.e., $\angle ACB = \frac{1}{2}\angle AOB$ & From statement (4), by whole part axiom. \\
\hline
\end{tabular}

\section*{Conclusion:-}

The inscribed angle is half of the central angle based on the same arc.

\section*{Relation between inscribed angle and its corresponding arc:-}

\section*{Proof:}
![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/412488ef-565e-4a71-b199-1e462d89fca7-17.jpg?height=543&width=486&top_left_y=303&top_left_x=230)

\section*{Given:-}

$O$ is the centre of the circle. The central angle $\angle AOB$ and the circumference angle $\angle ACB$ are based on the same arc $AB$.

\section*{To be proved:-}

$$2\angle ACB = \widehat{AB}$$

\section*{Construction:-}

Points $O$ and $C$ are joined.

\section*{Proof:}

In an isosceles triangle $OAC$,
$$\angle OAC + \angle OCA + \angle AOC = 180^{\circ}$$
or, $2\angle OCA = 180^{\circ} - \angle AOC$ ... (i)
[since $\angle OAC = \angle OCA$]

Again, in an isosceles triangle $OBC$,
$$\angle OCB + \angle OBC + \angle BOC = 180^{\circ}$$
or, $\angle OCB = 180^{\circ} - \angle BOC$ ... (ii)
[since $\angle OCB = \angle OBC$]

Adding equations (i) and (ii), we get,
$$2(\angle OCA + \angle OCB) = 360^{\circ} - (\angle AOC + \angle BOC)$$
or, $2\angle ACB = 360^{\circ} - \text{Reflex } \angle AOB$
or, $2\angle ACB = \angle AOB$
or, $2\angle ACB = \widehat{AB}$

Therefore, $2\angle ACB \cong \widehat{AB}$

\section*{Example 1}

In the given figure, $\angle PQR = 100^{\circ}$ and the points $P, Q$ and $R$ are the circumference points of the circle with the centre $O$. Find the value of $\angle OPR$.
![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/412488ef-565e-4a71-b199-1e462d89fca7-24.jpg?height=435&width=439&top_left_y=569&top_left_x=368)

\section*{Solution:-}

According to the figure, (i) Reflex angle $\angle POR = 2 \times \angle PQR$ [since the central angle and the inscribed angle based on the same arc $PR$.]
$$\text{Therefore, Reflex } \angle POR = 2 \times 100^{\circ} = 200^{\circ}$$

(ii) Reflex angle $\angle POR$ + Obtuse angle $\angle POR = 360^{\circ}$
[Sum of the angles around the point $O$]
$$\text{or, } 200^{\circ} + \text{obtuse } \angle POR = 360^{\circ}$$
$$\text{or, } \angle POR = 360^{\circ} - 200^{\circ} = 160^{\circ}$$

(iii) Again, $\triangle POR$ is an isosceles triangle. So that, $\angle OPR = \angle ORP$.
Now,
$$\angle OPR + \angle ORP + \angle POR = 180^{\circ}$$
[The sum of the angles of a triangle]
$$\text{or, } \angle OPR + \angle OPR + 160^{\circ} = 180^{\circ}$$
$$\text{or, } 2\angle OPR = 180^{\circ} - 160^{\circ} = 20^{\circ}$$
$$\text{or, } \angle OPR = \frac{20^{\circ}}{2}$$
$$\therefore \angle OPR = 10^{\circ}$$

\section*{Question no. 3:}

In the adjoining figure, $A, B, C$ and $D$ are four points on the circumference of the circle. The chords $AC$ and $BD$ are intersecting at a point $E$. If $\angle BEC = 140^{\circ}$ and $\angle ECD = 30^{\circ}$ find the value of $\angle BAC$.
![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/412488ef-565e-4a71-b199-1e462d89fca7-27.jpg?height=511&width=600&top_left_y=9&top_left_x=57)

\section*{Solution:}

(i) $\angle BEC = 140^{\circ}$ and $\angle ECD = 30^{\circ}$. We know, $\angle BEC + \angle CED = 180^{\circ}$ [The sum of the angles in straight line.]
$$\text{Or, } \angle CED = 180^{\circ} - \angle BEC$$
$$\text{Or, } \angle CED = 180^{\circ} - 140^{\circ} = 40^{\circ}$$
$$\therefore \angle CED = 40^{\circ}$$

(ii) Again,
$$\angle EDC + \angle CED + \angle ECD = 180^{\circ}$$
[The sum of the angles of a triangle]
$$\text{Or, } \angle EDC = 180^{\circ} - \angle CED - \angle ECD$$
$$\text{or, } \angle EDC = 180^{\circ} - 40^{\circ} - 30^{\circ}$$
$$\therefore \angle EDC = 110^{\circ}$$

(iii) Here, $\angle EDC = \angle BDC = 110^{\circ}$
Also,
$$\angle BAC = \angle BDC$$
[Inscribed angles standing on the same arc $BC$]
$$\therefore \angle BAC = 110^{\circ}$$

$$\angle PQR = 40^{\circ}$$

\section*{Question no. 7}
![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/412488ef-565e-4a71-b199-1e462d89fca7-32.jpg?height=589&width=515&top_left_y=109&top_left_x=122)
